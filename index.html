Research Summary

Do low-income households bear a greater distributional inflation burden when inflation is supply-driven versus demand-driven? Original econometric research using 25 years of monthly U.S. data.

Finding 1 — Core Result
A 1 ppt increase in supply-driven inflation widens the Q1–Q5 gap by 0.613 ppts. The same demand increase widens it by only 0.326 ppts. Supply shocks are 1.88× more regressive.
Finding 2 — Near-Zero Demand Effect
In demand-dominant months (N=115), the Q1–Q5 gap averages +0.025 ppts — economically negligible. In supply-dominant months (N=185): +0.579 ppts. Two-sample t=−6.32, p<0.001.
Finding 3 — COVID Structural Break
Both coefficients shifted significantly after Jan 2020 (Chow F=127.45). Supply fell 0.809→0.522; demand fell 0.644→0.248 — likely reflecting pandemic-era fiscal transfers buffering low-income households.
Finding 4 — Post-COVID Worst on Record
The 2021–2023 period shows a mean gap of +0.806 ppts — the largest in the 25-year sample, driven by supply shocks: food, energy, and supply-chain disruptions.

Supply vs. Demand PCE Decomposition

Shapiro (2022): each PCE category each month is classified supply-driven (price ↑, quantity ↓) or demand-driven (price ↑, quantity ↑). Source: Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco.

Supply-Driven vs. Demand-Driven PCE Inflation (ppts, y/y)
Hover for exact values · dominance classification · supply/demand ratio
The identification strategy: When prices rise alongside falling quantities, that's a supply constraint. When both rise together, that's a demand surge. The post-COVID period shows supply contributions roughly double their pre-pandemic average — confirming this was primarily a supply-shock episode.

Why it matters: When supply dominates (blue above red), this paper shows the distributional inflation gap is nearly 2× larger. The tooltip on each month tells you which type dominates and the ratio.

Global Supply Chain Pressure Index

NY Fed GSCPI measures global supply chain disruption in standard deviations above/below the historical mean. Used here as an independent supply shock instrument validating the Shapiro decomposition results.

NY Fed GSCPI (Jan 2000 – Feb 2026)
Hover for reading · stress level · distributional inflation implication
Peak: November 2021 — 4.49 SD above the historical mean. The highest reading in the dataset's history, coinciding exactly with the widening of the income inflation gap documented in this paper.

Robustness check: Using GSCPI > 1 SD as supply shock episodes yields mean gap +0.61 ppts vs. +0.29 ppts in low-GSCPI months — consistent with the Shapiro-based main results. The hover tooltip tells you the stress level and distributional implication for each month.

Impulse Response Functions (VAR)

Response of the Q1–Q5 inflation gap to a 1-SD supply vs. demand shock over 24 months. VAR(2) · p=2 by AIC/BIC/HQ/FPE · 500 bootstrap replications · 95% CI. Hover each point for the full context.

IRF: Inflation Gap Response — Supply vs. Demand Shock
Hover for month · response magnitude · CI bounds · significance status
How to read an impulse response function: The IRF shows how a variable responds over time after a one-standard-deviation shock hits the system at month 0. Think of it as a ripple in a pond — the shock is the stone, the inflation gap is the wave.

Supply shocks (blue): The gap jumps immediately at month 0 and stays statistically significant for ~6–10 months before returning to baseline. Supply disruptions remain in necessity prices for many months — food prices don't snap back quickly.

Demand shocks (red): Smaller initial response, confidence bands overlap zero throughout most of the horizon. Demand shocks have a lagged predictive relationship with the gap (Granger: F=3.40, p=0.001) but the dynamic distributional effect is diffuse rather than concentrated.

Hover tip: Each data point tooltip tells you whether that month's response is statistically significant at 95% or if the CI spans zero.

Historical Episode Analysis

Mean Q1–Q5 inflation gap across four macroeconomic episodes. Hover each bar for mean, SD, N, and plain-English interpretation of what drove the episode.

Mean Q1–Q5 Inflation Gap by Episode
Error bars = ±1 SD · hover each bar for full context and interpretation
COVID Onset (mean = −0.204): Only sustained negative-gap period in the sample. Energy/transport prices collapsed — these categories weigh more heavily in upper-income budgets — temporarily reversing the usual pattern.

GFC (mean = +0.335, SD = 1.41): High SD reflects extreme volatility — oil spiked to $145/barrel in July 2008 then crashed, creating wild swings in the energy-heavy low-income basket.

Post-COVID (mean = +0.806): The largest sustained gap in the sample — 2.5× the baseline. Food, energy, and supply-chain inflation hit the necessity-heavy low-income basket hardest, coinciding with GSCPI readings up to 4.49 SD.

Baseline (mean = +0.326): The persistent positive gap even in normal times reflects the structural basket difference — low-income households are always more exposed to necessity price fluctuations.
